<summary><b>Abstract</b> (click to expand)</summary>
<br>
South-facing apartments command a price premium in Chinese housing markets, a finding the hedonic literature treats as a stable characteristic value. We show that this premium moves with weather during the listing window. Using 217,640 Shanghai resale transactions from 2013 to 2020, cumulative extreme-heat exposure inside the listing period erodes the south-facing premium. The response is near zero in the first month of cumulative exposure, turns sharply negative once the count crosses about thirty days, and grows monotonically with the percentile threshold used to define an extreme-heat day. Restricting the window to a single summer raises the magnitude by roughly an order of magnitude. Sellers reduce the listing price by several times the erosion observed at the transaction price, while the listing-to-transaction discount rate does not respond, locating the pricing margin at the listing stage. The penalty is concentrated in low-rise buildings, which in Shanghai tend to be older walk-up stock with poorer insulation, and vanishes in buildings above eighteen floors. Triple interactions with humidity, wind, and cloud cover give signs consistent with thermal-comfort physics, and a placebo using north-facing units reverses sign. The pattern fits rational capitalization of cumulative cooling costs into the listing price rather than a sentiment shock at the moment of sale.
